I've been building software for more than 20 years, but the part that still interests me most is the beginning — when the problem is unclear, the constraints are moving, and nobody knows yet whether the idea will work.

That is where I do my best work: turning risky ideas into working systems, finding the simplest architecture that can survive reality, and implementing the parts where the real technical difficulty lives.

I care less about technology as identity and more about what can be built from it. Languages, runtimes, models, protocols, and infrastructure are tools — useful when every piece has a clear role.

## How I like to work

```text
unclear problem      → working prototype
risky assumption     → focused experiment
complex system       → explicit responsibilities
technical debt       → fix the foundation
"probably works"     → prove it
```

- I enjoy **0→1 engineering**, architecture, and the hardest implementation work.
- I prefer small, technically strong teams with trust, autonomy, and real ownership.
- I prototype quickly when learning — and care deeply about quality once a direction proves itself.
- I value direct feedback, technical honesty, and solutions that are easy to reason about.
- If an approach feels unnecessarily complicated, I will probably try to remove half of it.
